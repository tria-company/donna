## Section A: Attract

> *How To Get People To Engage, Who Otherwise Wouldn't, Using Free & Discount Promotions*

### The Origin Story

March 2016. I stood to the side of the stage. I looked back at the thousands in the crowd. No one knew me. I was 26 at the time. Most were older than me. I checked the clock. I was up soon. My heart thumped so loud I heard it in my ears.

I planned to talk about how I pre-sold each of my gyms to full capacity before opening, with no money out of pocket. I stepped on stage. My slides were bare -- black and white text with a few images. I felt silly seeing all the other speakers' professional presentations. And here I was, a kid in a neon green fitness t-shirt who had been broke only a few years before.

I explained the same process about our Free 6 Week Challenge -- how it worked and the numbers behind it. I told everyone exactly what we did, and how we did it. I'm pretty sure I ended my speech with "So yea, that's it."

When I got off the stage, I got mobbed. The talk transformed me from an unknown kid to someone worth listening to. People surrounded me several rows deep, firing questions: *Do you have a course? Do you have a coaching program? Can you do what you just went over at my gym? My friend has a gym, is there something of yours I can send him?*

It took me 60 minutes to make it from the door of the venue to the men's lavatory 30 feet away. And even there, someone followed me and asked me questions while I was peeing.

I walked away from the event with a pocket overflowing with business cards and over 100 new contacts. I had no idea what I was doing, but I knew I could probably help them.

So I created the first offer for what would later become Gym Launch by giving something away for free: me.

> *I'll fly out to your gym. I'll spend my own money on marketing, I'll work your leads. I'll close them. Then I get the cash that I collect, and I'll give you the customers for free. I'll show you how to sell supplements. I'll give you my nutrition program. I'll show you how to fulfill them. And I'll show you how to convert them into memberships. Again, for free. I only make what I sell.*

Sound like a Grand Slam Offer? Yep. I did this for 33 gyms and it took me 18 months of flying around the country. As you can imagine, it was easy to sell. After all, it was free. It's how I made my first million dollars (outside of my gyms). And I made it all, starting with a free offer.

### Why I Use Free & Discount Promotions

Whenever I get into a new market, I almost always start with something free or massively discounted. Reasons:

1. **I don't know what I'm doing yet** -- gives me wiggle room and forgiveness if it's rough around the edges
2. **Testimonials** -- fastest and easiest way is to work for free in exchange for them
3. **Conviction** -- if I haven't done something before, I tend to lack conviction; free or massive discounts help me close the first sales
4. **Referrals** -- when you start for free, it's easy to get referrals and start generating demand

**Get Flow. Monetize Flow. Then Add Friction.**

In almost every industry I profit from, I begin with a standalone offer, free offer, or a discount offer. They live at the core of my "get new clients" strategy.

I'm not saying you have to do this. I'm saying this is how I do it, and it has served me well. If you want to make money fast, ironically, many times it comes fastest from giving things away. Those who give the most, get the most.

**Important:** The point of creating a promotion is to enhance your Grand Slam Offer, not change it. Think of these like wrapping paper. You can wrap a gift in recycled newspaper or you can put it in a Chanel bag with matte black paper and a perfectly tied white bow. What's inside may be the same, but we are making it more inherently attractive. This is especially important when entering cold markets when you must give people a reason to move towards you.

## Premium Promotions: Presenting Your Grand Slam Offer On Its Own

> *"Some things in life are priceless. For everything else, there's Mastercard." -- Famous Advertising Campaign*

### The Power of Infinite Returns

I walked into the smoothie shop where I worked. My friend sat in the back leafing through a magazine. "You wouldn't believe this. This dude bought a burger for $50,000. That's insane."

The burger had gold flakes and exotic caviars on it. But what I remembered thinking was, "how much money did this guy make to be able to buy a $50,000 burger?" Some hedge fund manager making about $50 million dollars a year.

If he worked 2,000 hours in a year, he made $25,000/hr. It cost him two hours of his time to afford the burger. My own income of $6.75/hr -- two hours bought me a burger, fries, and a soda with tax from the place next door: $13.50. To him, given his income, it wasn't even insane. It was the same as me buying a burger.

That's when I realized the power of infinite returns. There is no cap on the upside, but you can only go down to zero when pricing.

### The Math

One business makes $100 per sale ($50 profit) and another makes $10,000 per sale ($9,500 profit). To make equivalent profits, business #1 needs to sell 190 people. Business #2 only needs one. If you offered the $10,000 offer to the same 190 prospects:

- 5% x 190 = 9.5 sales
- 9.5 sales x $9,500 profit = $90,250

Compared to:

- 190 sales x $50 profit = $9,500
- $90,250 / $9,500 = **9.5x as profitable**

And you only deal with nine or ten clients instead of 190.

**The big caveat:**

1. Have something very valuable to provide (your Grand Slam Offer)
2. Have a selling process that demonstrates that value

### If I Lost Everything And Had To Start Over

I would not start with a premium offer. I would add on a free or discount money model. Over time, as your reputation improves, you can remove those things. But in the beginning, they are essential for most. If you're new, start with a free or discount offer to get business in the door. Prove results. Then restructure to a premium offer afterwards.

You can also layer offers together. Premium offers work well as the "second" offer you give after a free offer. But they'll only work if you demonstrated value in the first offer.

### Pros of Premium Offers

**#1 Simplest math & fewest moving parts.** If you're only selling one core "thing," the math gets simple. You pay $XXXX for YY Leads/Calls/Appts, and you close Z sales @ $VVVV money.

**#2 Only "quality" customers -- decreases operational drag.** People who come in on premium offers are, in general, the most qualified customers. We "skim" with marketing and pay a premium for the cream instead of all the milk.

**#3 No discounts or incentives: high lead quality.** Anyone who comes in expects to be sold. Transparent intentions on both sides.

### Cons of Premium Offers

**#1 Most expensive cost per lead / can take more time to break even.** It costs a lot of money to get moving. Less room for error.

**#2 Must be a good copywriter and understand your avatar well.** Specificity is what gives copy its edge. "Cleaning the bathrooms yet again" beats "working hard." Free and Discount offers give more margin for error on copy.

**#3 Must be good at sales.** The conversion process end to end must be dialed in. You should already have a proven process or cash to figure one out.

**#4 Least efficient way to capture a marketplace.** A straight premium offer has to hit a lot of eyeballs before getting a bite.

**#5 Must have an extremely compelling offer.** Packed with bonuses, creative guarantees, and premium support to merit the higher cost.

### Conclusion

Premium offers are powerful. They can act as standalone offers that will make you money. If you have clients coming in already, using this new structure will allow you to 3-5x your prices without changing your services (seriously). But if you're just starting out, or your volume isn't high enough, or your cost of acquisition is higher than you can bear, then wrap your premium offer with a free or discount wrapper.

## Free Promotions

> *"If it's free, it's for me!"*

Free is the most powerful offer of all time and will never expire. At its base it is "something for nothing" or "value in advance." Dr. Dan Ariely demonstrated the "penny gap" -- 9x more people would take a free Hershey kiss than one sold for a penny.

If your free offer doesn't work, prospects either:

1. **Don't want your thing** -- change what you are giving away or how you describe it
2. **Don't believe you**
3. **Aren't actually seeing it** -- targeting issue (e.g., running a single ladies promotion to married moms)

A famous marketer tested believability: he ran newspaper offers saying "For every $100 you give me I will give you $1,000 back." No one ever responded. It was an amazing offer, but so good it was unbelievable. Whenever you give a crazy free offer, you must answer: **Why?** Give a good enough reason and people will believe you.

### Pros of Free Offers

**#1 Highest lead volume.** If we need volume, nothing works better than free. It gets the most leads per eyeball.

**#2 Lowest lead cost.** Same eyeballs, higher percentage raising hands = lower cost per raised hand.

**#3 Massive companies become massive & viral by learning to monetize "free."**
- Facebook: "Free to sign up and always will be"
- YouTube: "Watch & Create Videos for Free"
- Dropbox: "Free X Storage"
- Uber: "Free First Ride"
- Netflix: "Free 30 Days"
- Free Ear Piercings (Claire's)
- Free Wax (European Wax Center)
- Free Month (Public Storage)

### Cons of Free Offers

**#1 Volume can be a double-edged sword.** The key with free is learning to find the sweet spot on friction to maximize quality volume. Examples of friction:

1. **Increased qualifications:** "Must be over 25, employed, homeowner." Decreases volume, increases quality.
2. **Increased information requirements & question types:** 20-question application. Cell phone number or income questions create more friction than "first name." Multiple-choice creates less friction than open-ended.
3. **Increased number of steps:** A one-step email opt-in gets more people than a 5-step form. You want just enough to weed out the weirdos but not so much that you lose some lazy whales.
4. **Forced consumption:** Force a prospect to watch a 40-minute video before any CTA appears. Cuts volume but increases quality. Good for large audiences with cheap eyeballs.
5. **Advertisement length:** The length of ads/copy/videos before the CTA increases friction. A two-hour video vs. a 30-second video. Fewer clicks, but those clicks are worth more.

**#2 Some people have no intention of buying.** It still comes down to math:

| Scenario | Ad Spend | Leads | Qualified | Unqualified |
|----------|----------|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Campaign #1 (Free) | $1,000 | 500 | 250 (50%) | 250 (50%) |
| Campaign #2 (Premium) | $1,000 | 200 | 160 (80%) | 40 (20%) |

Your team may feel better about #2, but according to pure dollars and cents, #1 is better. Use friction to skim the cream off the top.

### Free Brings Broke People Myth

We've run four independent split tests of free versus premium offers. Each test had ten representative markets.

The gyms' sales closing percentage was **the same** between free and non-free respondents. Being "non-free" offered no advantage in close rates or average ticket size.

What wasn't equal? Volume and cost of leads. Going from non-free to a free front end **decreased lead costs by five times or more.**

That said, I'm not saying free is for every offer, every time. But if you learn how to harness it, there are ways to layer "free" into a powerful money model.

**Bottom line:** If I only had one offer to make to convert or my family would be killed, it would be a free offer. I'd rather wade through crappy leads, then figure out how to add friction, than look at an empty calendar.

## Discount Promotions

> *"The limit does not exist!" -- Cady Heron (Lindsay Lohan), Mean Girls*

### Understanding Discount Offers

"Free" and "discount" offers operate very similarly from a money model perspective -- they create a perceived value discrepancy that can drive action. I am not a big believer in "marginal" discounts (5-25% off). That's not enough to drive real behavior and basically just cuts into margin.

We're talking about **massive discounts (50% or more)**. Those drive action from a population that wouldn't otherwise act. A free or discount offer has to get people who wouldn't otherwise respond -- to respond.

Most of the time, a discount offer is a "piece of the thing," not the "entire thing."

### Four Ways To Display Discounts

Let's imagine an ultimate lemonade bundle: three bottles a day for $30 per day ($210/week). Four ways to promote:

1. **Percentage Off:** "New Client Special, 87% off first visit"
2. **Absolute Amount Off:** "$181 Off First Week" (Normally $210)
3. **Relative Equivalent Off:** "Save A Steak Dinner" (negative) or "Less Than Going Out To Lunch" (positive)
4. **Simply Discounted Price:** "$29 New Client Special"

Same exact promotion, communicated differently. Cycle through all four and test which resonates best.

### Pros of Discount Offers

**#1 Lots of cheap leads within the law.** In some countries, stipulations around "free" are forbidden. Discounts allow compliant advertising with decent lead volume.

**#2 You actually collect some money.** Won't amount to much, but it can help liquidate acquisition costs. Don't rely on it to fund marketing.

**#3 People come in expecting to spend.** More of a mental benefit for business owners/employees. Makes them more convicted in the selling process. Close rates on the initial discount tend to be higher, but the same on upselling the core offer.

**#4 The two-step sale (probably the biggest benefit).** Collect card over the phone. Eliminates no-shows (85-90%+ show rate). Sets up seamless future upsells. Example two-step sale:

> Ad --> Opt in --> Phone Call for $19 Promo, Set appt --> Shows up for $19 Appt --> Gets Value --> Schedules follow up appt for $2,100 treatment program sale --> Shows up and is sold.

**#5 Discount offers make upsells smooth as butter.** "Do you want to use the card you have on file?" People buy more when they don't have to take their card out. Amazon one-click purchasing, Disney money wristbands -- all designed to eliminate this friction.

### Cons of Discount Offers

**#1 Giving away the farm.** If you always discount your core offer, people become trained to buy only at discounted times. We're going to "splinter" our offer into tiny pieces and give a core component at a discount -- not the whole farm.

**#2 Bargain hoppers.** This was the issue with Groupon. People had no desire of buying the "main thing." But most businesses didn't know how to structure offers to qualify prospects. Don't see discount buyers as customers. See them as qualified leads. Resolve with intelligent offer structure.

### Discount Offers Conclusion

Discount offers can be used with great effectiveness on the front end or back end. As a front-end offer, discounts tend to work better with "well understood" services -- dentists, chiropractors, gyms, haircuts, etc. If a customer doesn't know what they are getting, a discount doesn't make sense because they have nothing to compare it to.

The other main benefit: discounts almost entirely eliminate no-shows.

## Attract Section Conclusion: Brass Tacks

If you are not getting enough response, you probably need to make your front end more appealing. We need to give people who otherwise wouldn't respond a reason to do so. Enhancing your Grand Slam Offer with a free or discount front end is the fastest way to do that.

**Always remember:** Generate Flow --> Monetize Flow --> Increase Friction. In that order.

Fundamentally, to make this process work you must know your business and your product better than your customers do. All businesses capitalize on an information advantage -- we know more about our customer's problem than they do. We capitalize on this advantage by making them aware of all the other problems they are going to encounter on their journey, then capitalizing on it through upsells. This is how you design a winning acquisition strategy.
