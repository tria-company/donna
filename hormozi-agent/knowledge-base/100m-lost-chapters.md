## Your First Avatar

> *"These aren't the droids you're looking for." -- Obi-Wan Kenobi, Star Wars: A New Hope*

> **Lost Chapter Author Note:** If I could go back in time, I would include this chapter in the $100M Offers book. It's incredibly important for figuring out who your ideal customer is. And no, this isn't yo mama's "find a niche" chapter. This is real, how-you-make-tons-of-money type stuff.

### The Vista Method

2019. The room was cold and dark. We sat at the "cool kids" table for business owners who had software over $10,000,000 ARR. Our software company ALAN had recently crossed the $1,700,000 per month threshold in its first six months.

The speaker broke down the process Vista (one of the most renowned software private equity funds in the world) used to grow companies. When they consider acquiring a company, they analyze the company's current customers. They look for the customers who stay the longest and pay the most. Then they score them according to this value. If they feel there's a vein of underserved valuable customers, they buy the company.

Once they buy a company, they cut channels that brought the low-value customers. Then they double down on the channels that brought in the best ones.

**That's it.** More of the high-profit customers. Fewer low-profit customers. Rinse. Repeat.

20% of customers bring in 80% of revenue. If you replace the 80% with those high spenders, you grow the business 5x.

### Finding the Right Customers: The Four Steps

**1) Survey your customers.** Set up a form with the questions below and send it out. Or go over it with them live. Ask every relevant detail:

- **Demographics:** Age? Gender? Political affiliation? Geographic location? Digital location? Single/Divorced? Partnered or solopreneur?
- **Business Stats (Before & Current):** Revenue? Profit? Number of employees? Churn? Pricing? Products? Customer lifetime value? Number of customers? Niche? How long in business?
- **Aspirations:** What was their goal upon purchasing? What problem were they trying to solve?
- **Buying Process:** Single biggest reason they bought? Trigger event? Specific content consumed? Specific testimonial? How many pieces consumed? When did they first hear about you vs. when they bought? Where did they first see you? Referral?

**2) Find your biggest spenders.** Sort the replies by the customers you like the most, spent the most, and stayed the longest. Focus on the top 20%. Ignore the rest.

**3) See what they have in common.** Read through all the answers and use your brain. Come up with the fewest qualifiers they all have in common. Usually three to five qualifiers.

**4) Execute.** Two important things:

- **Speak your new avatar.** Be up front about your customer requirements. Get all advertising to speak directly to them. Stop selling anyone who does not meet your ideal customer requirements. Increase effort on the channels these people come through.
- **Re-engineer the sales process.** Look at what caused these better customers to buy. Reverse-engineer the buying process your best customers went through. Then make it happen on purpose.

> **Pro Tip: You Make More Because of Who They Are, Not Because of Who You Are.** The profit you make comes from the premium you can charge. You can charge more because of who they are, rather than who you are. Quick Example: A sales page designer improves conversion from 5% to 7%. Company A makes $100K/mo from the page -- now makes $140K/mo. Company B makes $10M/mo -- now makes $14M/mo. Same work. Charge 10% of growth: $40K/year from A, $4M/year from B.

> **Pro Tip: What To Do If You Have No Customers -- Start With What You Know.** The best VCs invest in founders with past experience relative to the industry they want to serve. Start with the industry you know the most about. Create a narrow target, then serve them first. Run the customer analysis process again once you have more customers to survey.

### The Gym Launch Case Study

**Findings from Steps 1-3 about our best customers:**

- **Demographics:** Right leaning/conservative, married, 25-45, male, gym owner, US-based
- **Business Requirements:** Signed lease, 1+ employees minimum, $10,000+ per month revenue, minimum 30 existing clients
- **Aspirations:** $1M+ gym, not work so much, open more locations
- **Buying Reasons:** Not enough leads, "bad market," bad pricing, can't find good employees

**Step 4a -- New Redefined Avatar:** We focused on the audiences with the highest concentration of these types. Spelled out requirements in ads and pages. Talked only about specific problems and aspirations of best customers.

**Findings about how they bought:** 78% of top customers consumed AT LEAST TWO pieces of long-form content before purchasing.

**Step 4b -- Reverse-engineered buying process:** Injected two long-form high-value content pieces to each lead. Increased total content output. Created a list of "all-time greatest hits" for the sales team. They hand-selected 2-3 pieces per prospect, forcing them through the same buying process that caused best customers to buy.

### A Comparison

A competitor serving the same vertical, making the same number of total sales, was making 70x less profit. They accepted anyone with a pulse and a credit card -- high churn, high CAC, low retention, lower satisfaction. We selectively pursued the highest value customers. Same market. Different customer segmentation. Monstrously different results.

### Quality Over Quantity

Every time we removed qualification steps, our lead volume increased, but we made less money. Merging marketing and sales into one acquisition department solved this for good.

The average Gym Launch competitor has an LTGP of around $6,000-$8,000. Our LTGP is north of $45,000. Despite being "only" 6-8x higher, the subsequent profits are breathtakingly different.

> **Pro Tip: Sell To People Who Don't Stop Buying.** Fortunes are created when we sell things that customers don't stop buying. Enterprise clients tend to get higher multiples because they deal with better customers who can afford more, have resources to deploy, and once they start buying, tend to continue to buy.

### Three Actions

1. Survey your existing customers
2. Decide which characteristics are leading indicators of high-value customers
3. Change your messaging in advertising and re-engineer a sales process for them

### The Origin Story

March 2016. I stood to the side of a huge marketing stage. I was 26. I talked about how I pre-sold each of my gyms to full capacity before opening, with no money out of pocket. When I got off stage, I got mobbed. The talk transformed me from an unknown kid to someone worth listening to.

I created the first offer for what would become Gym Launch by giving something away for free: me.

> *I'll fly out to your gym. I'll spend my own money on marketing, I'll work your leads. I'll close them. Then I get the cash that I collect, and I'll give you the customers for free. I'll show you how to sell supplements. I'll give you my nutrition program. I'll show you how to fulfill them. And I'll show you how to convert them into memberships. Again, for free. I only make what I sell.*

I did this for 33 gyms over 18 months. It's how I made my first million dollars outside of my gyms. Starting with a free offer.

### Why I Use Free & Discount Promotions

Whenever I get into a new market, I almost always start with something free or massively discounted. Reasons:

1. I don't know what I'm doing yet -- gives wiggle room if it's rough around the edges
2. I like having testimonials -- fastest way is to work for free in exchange for them
3. I lack conviction on new things -- free or discounted helps me close the first sales
4. Easy to get referrals and start generating demand

**Get Flow. Monetize Flow. Then Add Friction.**

**Important:** The point of creating a promotion is to enhance your Grand Slam Offer, not change it. Think of these like wrapping paper making a gift more inherently attractive.

### Premium Promotions: Presenting Your Grand Slam Offer On Its Own

> *"Some things in life are priceless. For everything else, there's Mastercard." -- Famous Advertising Campaign*

#### The Power of Infinite Returns

A hedge fund manager making $50M/year buys a $50,000 burger. At $25,000/hr, it cost him two hours. At $6.75/hr, two hours bought me a burger, fries, and soda -- $13.50. There is no cap on the upside, but you can only go down to zero when pricing.

**The math:** Business #1 makes $100/sale ($50 profit), Business #2 makes $10,000/sale ($9,500 profit). Same 190 prospects:

- Business #1: 190 sales x $50 profit = $9,500
- Business #2: 5% x 190 = 9.5 sales x $9,500 = $90,250
- Result: 9.5x as profitable

#### If I Lost Everything And Had To Start Over

I would NOT start with a premium offer. I would add a free or discount money model. Earlier in my career, all my offers were free. Start with free or discount, prove results, then restructure to premium.

#### Pros of Premium Offers

1. **Simplest math & fewest moving parts** -- One core thing, simple accounting
2. **Only "quality" customers -- decreases operational drag** -- Most qualified customers, less "sifting through the poo"
3. **No discounts or incentives: high lead quality** -- Transparent intentions on both sides

#### Cons of Premium Offers

1. **Most expensive cost per lead / can take more time to break even** -- Need cash or skill to drive volume
2. **Must be a good copywriter and understand your avatar well** -- Specificity gives copy its edge
3. **Must be good at sales** -- Conversion process must be dialed in
4. **Least efficient way to capture a marketplace** -- Low volume despite high value
5. **Must have an extremely compelling offer** -- Packed with bonuses, creative guarantees, premium support

#### Conclusion

Premium offers are powerful standalone offers. They can 3-5x your prices without changing services. But if you're starting out or volume isn't high enough, wrap your premium offer with a free or discount wrapper.

### Free Promotions

> *"If it's free, it's for me!"*

Free is the most powerful offer of all time. Dr. Dan Ariely demonstrated the "penny gap" -- 9x more people would take a free Hershey kiss than one sold for a penny.

If your free offer doesn't work, prospects either:

1. Don't want your thing -- change what you give away or how you describe it
2. Don't believe you
3. Aren't actually seeing it (targeting issue)

#### Pros of Free Offers

1. **Highest lead volume** -- Gets the most leads per eyeball
2. **Lowest lead cost** -- Higher percentage of raised hands = lower cost per hand
3. **Massive companies become massive by learning to monetize "free"** -- Facebook, YouTube, Dropbox, Uber, Netflix, Claire's, European Wax Center, Public Storage

#### Cons of Free Offers

**#1 Volume can be a double-edged sword.** Examples of friction to increase quality:

1. **Increased qualifications** -- "Must be over 25, employed, homeowner"
2. **Increased information requirements & types of questions** -- 20-question application; cell phone and income questions create more friction than first name
3. **Increased number of steps** -- Each step drops prospects, yielding fewer but higher quality
4. **Forced consumption** -- Force prospects to watch a 40-minute video before any CTA appears
5. **Advertisement length** -- Longer ads/copy/videos before CTA increase lead quality

**#2 Some people have no intention of buying.** But it always comes down to math:

| Scenario | Ad Spend | Leads | Qualified | Unqualified |
|----------|----------|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Free Offer | $1,000 | 500 | 250 | 250 |
| Premium Offer | $1,000 | 200 | 160 | 40 |

Team may feel better about #2, but #1 produces more qualified leads in absolute terms.

#### Free Brings Broke People Myth

We ran four independent split tests of free vs. premium offers. Each test had 10 representative markets. Result: **Close rate was the same.** Being "non-free" offered no advantage in close rates or average ticket size. But going from non-free to free decreased lead costs by 5x or more.

> **Pro Tip: Free Makes More Money.** People want to run a premium offer at "free offer" prices -- that's where you get messed up. If lead cost is 5-10x higher for premium, prices should be at least 5-10x higher. Price, Prospect, Process, Promotion, and Product should ALL reflect the offer structure. Free beats premium hands down unless you've truly mastered high-ticket selling.

**Bottom line:** If I only had one offer to make to convert or my family would be killed, it would be a free offer.

### Discount Promotions

> *A discount turns wants into haves. Why pay more when you could pay less?*

I am not a big believer in "marginal" discounts (5-25% off). We're talking about massive discounts (50% or more) -- the types that drive action from a population that wouldn't otherwise act.

Most of the time, a discount offer is a "piece of the thing," not the "entire thing."

#### Four Ways To Display Discounts

Example: Ultimate lemonade bundle, three bottles a day for $30/day ($210/week). Promote four ways:

1. **Percentage Off:** "New Client Special, 87% off first visit"
2. **Absolute Amounts Off:** "$181 Off First Week" (Normally $210)
3. **Relative Equivalent Off:** "Save A Steak Dinner" (negative) or "Less Than Going Out To Lunch" (positive)
4. **Simply the Discounted Price:** "$29 New Client Special"

Cycle through all four and test which resonates best.

> **Pro Tip:** Use absolute prices when talking about well-understood products and services. If you discount something people don't understand, the offer won't be effective.

#### Pros of Discount Offers

1. **Lots of cheap leads within the law** -- In some countries "free" with stipulations is forbidden; discounts allow compliant advertising
2. **You actually collect some money** -- Helps liquidate acquisition costs (but don't rely on it)
3. **People come in expecting to spend** -- More of a mental benefit for the salesperson's conviction
4. **The two-step sale (probably the biggest benefit)** -- Collect card on phone, eliminates no-shows (85-90%+ show rate), sets up seamless future upsells
5. **Discount offers make upsells smooth** -- "Do you want to use the card you have on file?" Amazon one-click, Disney wristbands -- eliminating friction sells more

> **Pro Tip: It's Easier To Upsell Than To Sell.** Once you get someone to buy even a small item, the likelihood they buy again goes way up. "Foot in the door" principle.

> **Author Note: Add Steps As Price And Complexity Increase.** The more complex or expensive the thing, the more time a prospect needs with you before buying.

> **Pro Tip: Give Away Lower Cost Time If You Can.** Have a front desk admin or assistant handle the first visit. The doctor only sees the most-qualified candidates. I call this the Proprietary 5 Min Appointment Method.

#### Cons of Discount Offers

1. **Giving away the farm** -- If you always discount your core offer, people become trained to buy only at discounted times. "Splinter" your offer into tiny pieces and give a core component at a discount -- not the whole farm.
2. **Bargain hoppers** -- Don't see discount buyers as customers. See them as qualified leads. Resolve with intelligent offer structure.

#### Attract Section Conclusion: Brass Tacks

If you are not getting enough response, make your front end more appealing. Always remember: **Generate Flow --> Monetize Flow --> Increase Friction.** In that order.

All businesses capitalize on an information advantage. We know more about our customers' problems than they do. We capitalize by making them aware of problems they'll encounter on their journey, then capitalizing through upsells.

## Section B: The Expensive Customer Problem

> *If you can spend more than your competition to get a customer, you will get more customers than them.*

> **Lost Chapter Author Note:** These chapters were in the final draft of $100M Money Models and I cut them last minute because I thought they were too conceptual. But I love these chapters. Pay extra attention. Big money comes from this small section.

### Customer Financed Acquisition (CFA)

Customers cost money. If you make that money back faster, you can get customers faster.

**CFA** is when 30 days of gross profit from a customer is greater than the CAC:

```
30 Day Gross Profit > CAC
```

But what if we could do better?

```
30 Day GP > 2x CAC
```

2x is my "real life" minimum standard. If you do it that way, you only have to buy your first customer. Then that customer pays for every other customer.

You pull **three levers** to make CFA work.

---

### The Three Levers of CFA

To grow a business: Get more customers OR Make them worth more. Plus one more variable: **speed.**

1. **Get More Customers --> Lower CAC**
2. **Make Them Worth More --> Increase LTGP**
3. **Do It Fast --> Decrease Payback Period (PPD)**

#### Lever #1: Lower CAC

CAC = (Money spent on advertising + sales + supporting activities) / Number of customers acquired

**Example:** You pay a content producer $10,000/month. You acquire 10 new customers from their content. CAC = $10,000 / 10 = $1,000

#### Lever #2: Increase Lifetime Gross Profit (LTGP)

**Gross Profit (GP):** Price minus cost of fulfillment.

- **Product Example:** Widget sells for $100, costs $20 to make/ship. GP = $80.
- **Service Example:** 10 packages at $1,000 each. Employee costs $2,000. Total GP = $8,000. GP per customer = $800.

#### Lever #3: Decrease Payback Period

If a customer pays for themselves today, you can get another one tomorrow. If it takes 30 days, you wait 30 days. Speed matters.

**Bottom Line:** The lower your CAC, the faster you break even. The higher your GP, the faster you get cash flow and grow. Do both -- fast.

---

### Cost To Acquire a Customer (CAC)

Most entrepreneurs have never calculated their actual CAC. That $1,000 sale you thought cost $200 really costs $500. That can be the difference between $1M/month and $10M/month.

**Three examples:**

**a) Outreach Example:**
- $200/mo email software + $3,000/mo emailer + $800 commissions (8 x $100) = $4,000
- CAC = $4,000 / 8 customers = **$500**

**b) Content Marketing Example:**
- Media payroll: $5,000 x 2 = $10,000 + $1,000 commissions = $11,000
- CAC = $11,000 / 10 customers = **$1,100**

**c) Paid Ads Example:**
- $4,000 media buyer + $20,000 ad spend + $1,000 software + $10,000 commissions = $35,000
- CAC = $35,000 / 10 customers = **$3,500**

**CAC Action Step:** Calculate your CAC for the past few months, by channel. One of the first things we do when investing is run a full diagnostic. Half the time we find a channel doing significantly better than others.

---

### Lifetime Gross Profit (LTGP)

**LTGP:** Total gross profit collected over the lifespan of a customer.

> **Important:** Many entrepreneurs mix up Gross Profit and Net Profit. Gross Profit is money left after subtracting costs of making/delivering. Net Profit is money left after subtracting ALL costs.

#### LTGP Step One: Gross Profit

- **Product Example:** $100 widget - $20 costs = $80 GP. Gross margin = 80%.
- **Service Example:** 10 clients at $3,000/mo, rep costs $6,000/mo. Revenue = $30,000. GP = $24,000. Gross margin = 80%. GP per customer = $2,400.

**Action:** Figure out your gross profit and gross margin for each thing you sell.

#### LTGP Step Two: Average Transactions per Lifetime

1. Export lifetime customer data, sort by transactions, average the column (e.g., Avg = 4)
2. For recurring revenue: use **churn** -- the percentage of customers that leave between periods
   - Last period: 100 customers, this period: 95 of the original. Churn = 5/100 = 5%
   - Note: New signups during the period do NOT affect churn

**Action:** Figure out number of transactions or churn rate.

#### LTGP Step Three: Calculate

- **Physical Products:** GP x Avg Transactions = LTGP --> $80 x 4 = **$360 LTGP**
- **Services (recurring):** GP / Churn = LTGP --> $2,400 / 5% = **$48,000 LTGP**

**LTGP is the arms race of business.** You can only get CAC to zero, but LTGP can go infinitely high. It's easier to make advertising more efficient than to make people stay longer.

### Payback Period (PPD)

> *How fast do you make your money back?*

**Payback Period:** The time it takes to break even on what you spent to acquire a customer. Technically, it's when GP > CAC.

**Example:** $50/mo GP, $100 CAC. Day 1: $50 back. Day 31: another $50. PPD = 31 days.

#### Alex's Most Prized Metric: 30 Day Cash (30D Cash)

**30D Cash** = gross profit extracted from a new customer in their first 30 days.

Why 30 days? Any business can get interest-free financing for 30 days (credit cards). If 30D Cash > CAC, you get free customers using other people's money.

**The Process:**

1. **Day 0:** Borrow $40 to acquire first customer
2. **Day 1-30:** Make $50 revenue, $10 fulfillment cost, $40 GP remains
3. **Day 30:** Repay original $40 debt with $40 GP. Balance = $0. Re-borrow $40 for next customer.
4. **Day 30-60:** First customer generates another $40 GP -- pocket it
5. **Day 60:** Second customer generates $40 GP, repay debt. Now have 2 customers paying $80 GP/month, zero debt.

#### Example: Normal Lemonade Business (The Problem)

$10/mo price, $2/mo fulfillment, $8/mo GP, $20 CAC, 5-month lifespan.

- Day 0: -$20 (marketing spend)
- Day 30: -$12 ($8 GP towards CAC)
- Day 60: -$4 (still recouping)
- Day 90: +$4 (finally making money)
- Day 120: +$12
- Day 150: +$20 (client cancels)

Hard business to grow. Don't see cash returned until month 3.

#### Example: Wonderful Lemonade Business (The Solution)

Same prices/margins, but CAC = $1, PPD = 7 days.

- Day 0: -$1 marketing
- Day 7: +$7 (acquire customer, $8 GP, pay back $1 CAC, $7 left)
- Day 8: $0 (spend $7 on 7 more customers)
- Day 14: +$56 (7 customers x $8 GP each)
- Day 15: +$49 (spend $7 to get 7 more, keep $49)
- Day 22: +$105 (another 7 customers = +$56)
- Day 37: +$113 (first customer renews = +$8 for free)
- Day 44: +$169 (next 7 renew = +$56)
- Day 51: +$225 (next 7 renew = +$56)

**Total marketing spend: $1.** The rest is playing with "found money."

**CFA Level 1:** GP < CAC in first 30 days. You eventually come out ahead, but it requires life savings, loans, credit. It sucks.

**CFA Level 2:** GP = CAC in first 30 days. Your credit limit becomes your advertising budget. You can expand by paying balance early, higher limits, or more cards.

**CFA Level 3:** GP > 2x CAC in first 30 days. You can double your business every month. All customers pay for themselves.

**Basic Outline:** The back end informs the front end. The more money you make per customer, the more you can spend to acquire customers. This section shows how to "stack" Grand Slam Offers on top of one another to create higher lifetime value.

### Back End: The Value Grid

> *The business owner who makes his customer more valuable to his business than to that of his competition, wins.*

Winter 2019. On a $50,000 consulting day: "You sell twice as many people as I do but make 56x more profit?" They had one offer that wasn't irresistible, no upsell or continuity. It cost them 50% more to acquire customers while I made 10x more on the same customer.

**LTGP is the arms race of business.** If you can pay 10x more to acquire a customer than your competition, you can make yourself virtually unbeatable -- sucking up the marketplace and making it difficult for new entrants.

#### The Grid vs. The Stair Step

I think of increasing LTV as a **grid**, not a stair step:

- Customers don't buy linearly. They skip offers, buy only the ones they want.
- The grid makes numbers visual -- add up revenue from all clients, divide by total prospects to know your LTV.

**Simple Value Grid:** 10 prospects, 8 take trial, 3 convert to paid. Total: $600 from 10 prospects. 30D Cash = $75.

**After Offer Stacking:** Same business type. 30D Cash = $1,763. That's a ~14-15x difference. If your competitor can pay $100/lead, you could pay $1,500/lead and remain equally profitable.

---

### Advanced Offer Stacking: How To

> *"The amount of money you make is directly proportional to the amount of goodwill you have multiplied by the amount of offers you make." -- Frank Kern*

**Warning:** Adding offers adds operational complexity. Look for things that add revenue with little to no cost.

#### Identify Adjacent Customer Needs

Look at all revenue streams related to the core desires you solve. Create affiliate relationships or add offerings with little operations. Over $3,000,000 in pure-profit affiliate commissions at time of writing.

#### Ultimate Offer Stacking Process

```
1) Attract --> 2) Up Front Cash --> 3) Upsell/Downsell --> 4) Continuity
```

Repeat the cycle:

```
... --> 4) Continuity --> 5) Continuity #2 --> 6) Up Front Cash #2
```

#### Sample Weight Loss Offer Flow

**Sale #1: Service Sale (Up Front Cash)**
- Offer high-ticket solution first
- If no: half down, different payment plan
- If no: quarter down, higher payment plan
- If no: shortened duration, quarter down only
- If no: free trial, card on file
- If no: complementary nutrition orientation (leads to Sale #2)

**Sale #2: Physical Products Sale (Upsell)**
- At orientation: sell 3-month supplement bundle
- If no: 1-month supply on subscription
- If no: just the essentials
- If no: 1-2 must-take products
- Then: offer meal prep services (affiliate relationship)

> **Pro Tip: Free Onboarding.** The cash from products sold at orientation covered payroll to onboard every customer one-on-one. Trainers made more per hour than personal training. Customers loved it. It covered entire cost of acquisition.

**Sale #3: More Services (Continuity)**
- "Feedback" meeting a few weeks later
- If happy: sell on staying long-term (high ticket prepayment, downsell to continuity)
- If unhappy: sell higher level program with more support. "Every problem is an upsell opportunity."

**Sale #4: Prepay Sale or Last Chance (Up Front Cash)**
- Get them to prepay for services, or last chance at continuity before program ends

#### Four Steps To Picking The Right Offer

1. **Right Stage** -- Attraction? 30-Day GP? Lifetime GP?
2. **Right Problem** -- Pick problems you can solve with existing resources
3. **Right Way** -- Solve based on customer's preference, not yours
4. **Right Time** -- Offer at the moment of greatest need

### Attraction Offer: Free Presentations

Presentations work for complex and expensive products for three reasons: (1) captive audience, (2) long enough to explain and provide value, (3) offer at peak motivation.

#### Examples with Benchmarks

- **Free XYZ Dinner:** 60-90 min presentation during meal. Aim: 25% take lower offer, 33% of those take higher offer.
- **Free 90-min Masterclass:** Remote. Aim: 10% conversion.
- **Free 5-Day Challenge:** 4 live presentations Mon-Thu, offer on Friday. Aim: 2-5% of signups.
- **Free 3-Day Virtual Summit:** Multiple speakers over 3 days. Pitch end of day 2 and day 3. Aim: 10% of final day attendees.
- **Free Strategy Call:** BANT qualification (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing). Aim: 25% of those reaching second call.

#### Three Core Limiting Beliefs

1. **Results must come easy** -- Easy to use, not frustrating, you can do it
2. **I must get the best results possible** -- Past failures weren't your fault
3. **Results must lead to attention and approval (status)** -- This positions you above others

**Rule:** The more time they spend with you, the more money they'll spend with you.

### Attraction Offer: Freemium

> **Note:** Mostly for software or media businesses with ~100% incremental margins. Not recommended without large capital.

**Freemium is not a business model -- it is an acquisition strategy.** Give something so valuable people use it continually for free. They must come from word of mouth. Upsell a percentage into paid tiers.

**Examples:** Dropbox, Spotify, Wistia, Gmail storage.

Requirements: (1) costs almost nothing to fulfill, (2) provides continuous value, (3) doesn't give so much they never upgrade.

**Roadblocks:** Conversion too low, not valuable enough to spread virally, fulfillment costs too high.

---

### Attraction Offer: Free Pick Your Price

Market as free. At checkout, offer pick-your-price with three tiers of bonuses. If someone doesn't pay, give them the basic level free but still upsell other products/services.

**Examples:** Free car wash (donations for staff, bonuses at $30/$67/$99+). Free weight loss (most people pay $99, tiers at $99/$199/$499). Free coaching (tiers with escalating access).

> **Pro Tip: Paid Version** -- An art gallery used price ranges per piece. "This painting is between $149 and $299." Most people pay above the halfway point because they don't want to seem cheap.

---

### Upsell Offer: Free With Alternate Revenue Stream

Give one service/product free, upsell something else.

- **Paired Upsell:** Give thing A free in exchange for buying thing B
- **Independent Upsell:** Give thing A free, encourage buying thing B

**Examples:** Free storage + sell locks. Free 28-day program + $400 supplements. Free PT + orthotics/bands. Free coaching + software monetization. Free book + shipping + audio + templates + training upsells.

**Key insight:** If someone does not buy the upsell, they are unlikely to stay. It's the greatest predictor of back-end conversion. A must-have, not a nice-to-have.

### Continuity Offer: Lifetime Upgrades -- Get Them To Stick

A good offer got them to start. Good bonuses get them to stick.

#### Bonus Framework: "When" and "What"

**When:** Delay (time-based) or Milestone (action/achievement-based)

**What:** One-time bonus, Variable bonus (changes each time), or Lifetime upgrade (permanent)

#### Important Notes

- Tell customers about bonuses (they can only stay longer if they know)
- Keep the exact bonus a surprise for flexibility
- Variable > static (customers get used to anything)
- Give status labels when they unlock bonuses: "VIP," "committed," "lifer," "elite"
- Celebrate status changes publicly
- Combine bonuses: delay + milestone, one-time + recurring

---

### Continuity Offer: Lifetime Discounts -- Get Them To Stick

Cheaper price as long as they stay on recurring payments. If they leave, they can't get it back. Add urgency and scarcity.

**Example:** "The Opener" -- opened gyms for a billion-dollar franchise with 400+ members. 14-day trial into lifetime "founding member" discount. 80% sign up. They stick because they don't want to lose the discount.

#### Important Notes

- **Know your numbers.** Costs change. Locked-in rates can become unprofitable.
- **Three display methods:** Percentage off, dollar amount off, fixed price
- **Limit to fixed periods** for flexibility
- **Never waste a crisis** -- use real life events as reasons
- **The Bigger The Head, The Longer The Tail** -- Larger up-front payments lead to longer retention (Sunk Cost Fallacy)

---

### Continuity Offer: Discount + One-Time Fee

Discounted first period rate + one or more "made up" fees. Fees liquidate commissions and acquisition costs.

**Example:** 95% off first month ($100) + $1,900 setup fee = $2,000 Day 1, then recurring.

**Key principle:** The higher the one-time startup fee, the lower the churn.

**Four steps:** (1) Pick fee name, (2) Pick fee price, (3) Pick "reason why," (4) Start charging, discounting, or waiving it.

### Training: The 3Ds

1. **Document:** Make a checklist of exactly how you do it. Test: can you do A+ work following only the directions?
2. **Demonstrate:** Walk them through step by step. Adjust where they slow down.
3. **Duplicate:** They do it following the checklist. Fix checklist until it works.

**Training Notes:**
- If they get it wrong, WE got it wrong
- Focus on ability to follow directions, not just results
- Praise successful steps. Don't take over on mistakes.
- Fix one step at a time
- "Slow then smooth then fast"

### The Performance Diamond

When performance drops, diagnose with four reasons:

1. **Communication** -- They don't know THAT we want them to do it. Have them describe their job back to you.
2. **Training** -- They don't know HOW. Have them demonstrate the checklist.
3. **Motivation** -- They don't WANT to. Check: rewards, aversions, outside events.
4. **Circumstances** -- Something is stopping them. "We're out of beef." "Phone broke."

> **Pro Tip (from Leila):** "Over the last [time period] your [performance] has changed from the norm. What do you think has gotten in the way and how can I help?"

### Calculating Returns

- Total Payroll / Total Engaged Leads = Cost per Engaged Lead
- If 1 in 10 becomes customer: CAC = Cost/Lead x 10
- LTGP:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better = good shape
- If CAC > 3x industry average: diagnose advertising vs. sales problem

## Maker or Manager?

### Two Types of Calendars

**Managers:** 30-60 minute blocks, 10-20 per day. Empty slot = lost opportunity. Fill all chunks.

**Makers:** Half-day or full-day blocks, 1-2 per day, 10-14 per week. Cannot make in small blocks. A 30-min meeting breaks a morning into two unusable sub-blocks. The Zeigarnik effect (open loops eat attention) makes pending meetings destroy productivity even before they happen.

### The Problem

When a manager asks a maker for "just 30 minutes," it costs the maker 10x more. Worse: the worse performance gets from interruptions, the more the manager checks in. Lose-lose.

### The Solution

**To managers:** (1) Understand both costs (coordination disruption + lost work block), (2) Don't take offense at "no"

**To makers:** (1) Let managers help you -- share this framework, (2) Make meeting blocks, schedule from back to front, (3) Set expectations for slow responses during maker's block, (4) Work when you say you work

> **Pro Tip for Solopreneurs:** Work 4am-10am on maker work, tend to fires during day. Nine work blocks per week.

**To organizations:** (1) Mandate "quiet" time chunks, (2) Spread awareness of both working styles
